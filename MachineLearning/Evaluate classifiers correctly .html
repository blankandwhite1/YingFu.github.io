Evaluate classifiers correctly

In general, classifiers are commonly evaluated using either a numeric metric, such as accuracy, or a graphical representation of performance, such as ROC curve or PR curve.

Sometimes some metrics evaluated indicates that the classifier performs quite well in a task, one may be very happy and will think that your machine learning algorithm is doing an excellent job. But does it really perform well? For example, in medical diagnostic tasks, if the disease is rare, predicting all the individuals will be negative will reach a high accuracy, but is this classifier really useful for diagnostic?

It is important to understand both what a classification metric expresses and what it hides. This blog intends to examine some common classifier metrics and discuss the pitfalls of relying on single metric.

1. Some metrics calculated from confusion matrix

Classification metrics are calculated from true positive}(TPs), false positives(FPs), false negatives(FNs) and true negatives(TNs), all of which are tabulated in the so-called confusion matrix.

In different tasks, the focus of these four quantities differs. For instance, in medical diagnostic tasks, the consequences of misdiagnosing a disease as no disease are higher than the misdiagnosis of a disease as a diseas, so FNs would be important than FPs.

Some common metrics calculated from confusion matrix are:

Accuracy is the measure of the probability that your estimate is 0 or 1 while its true label is also 0 or 1. It is a measure of how many samples have been identified correctly.

Sensitivity/Recall/TPR is the probability that your estimate is 1 given all the samples whose true class label is 1. It is a measure of how many of the positive samples have been identified as being positive.

Specificity/TNR is the measure of the probability that your estimate is 0 given all the samples whose true class label is 0. It is a measure of how many of the negative samples have been identified as being negative.

Precision is the measure of the probability that a sample is a true positive class given that your classifier said it is positive. It is a measure of how many of the samples predicted by the classifier as positive is indeed positive. (Note: precision depends on how rare is the positive class. In other words, it is used when positive class is more interesting than the negative class)

2. Same value of a metric corresponds different classifier performance

Figures below shows that the same value of a metric can correspond to very different classifier performance.

In (a-d), each panel shows three different classification scenarios with a table of corresponding values of accuracy(ac), sensitivity(sn), precision(pr), score() and Matthews correlation coefficient(MCC). Scenarios in a group have the same value(0.8) for the metric in blod in each table: (a) accuracy, (b) sensitivity/recall, (c) precision and (d) score.

In each panel, those observations that do not contribute to the corresponding metric are struck through with a red line.

In (a), although the accuracy is the same, what the first and second scenarios show are while in imbalanced classes situation, especially when the positive or negative observations are rare, predicting all the observations are positive or negative can reach a high accuracy, which is useless at all. However, in a balanced classes situation, accuracy is useful.

In (b), the sensitivity/recall of three scenarios remains the same. Just look a little bit of the formula of how sensitivity/recall is calculated, you will find that it only depends on TPs and FNs, so once TPs and FNs are fixed, the sensitivity/recall is fixed. Neither TNs nor FPs affect this metric, and a classifier that simply predicts that all data points are positive has high sensitivity.

In (c), the precision of three scenarios remains the same. Just look a little bit of the formula of how precision is calculated, you will find that it only depends on TPs and FPs, so once TPs and FPs are fixed, the precision is fixed and neither TNs and FNs are captured.

One approach that can capture all the data in the confusion matrix is the Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC).

The MCC is in essence a correlation coefficient between the observed and predicted binary classifications; it returns a value between −1 and +1. A coefficient of +1 represents a perfect prediction, 0 no better than random prediction and −1 indicates total disagreement between prediction and observation.

3. ROC curve and PR curve

In fact, almost all classifier generate positive or negative predictions by applying a threshold to a score. And a higher threshold will make predictions to be positive more carefully and will reduce false positive rate.

The ROC curve (figure below(a)) is created by plotting the true positive rate(TPR)/recall/sensitivity against the false positive rate (FPR) as its discrimination threshold is varied. A good classifier should aim to reach as close to the top left corner as possible.

Another visualization is the Precision-recall(PR) curve (figure belwo (b) ), which polts the Precision against the Recall as its discrimination threshold is varied. Its interpretation is slightly different, as the best classifier would be as close to the top right as possible, gaining the best trade-off of recall and precision.

In the figure below, A is a good classifier and B is similar to a random classification and C is even worse than random.

Summary metrics of these two graphs are also used: the area under the curve (AUC) for the ROC curve and the area under the PR curve (AUPRC). The larger AUC and AUPRC is, classifier performs better.

 

Class imbalance can cause ROC curves to be poor visualization of classifier performance.

ROC and PR curve for two data sets with very different class balance are shown below: (a) 5% positive and (b) 50% positive observations. For each panel, observations are shown as vertical lines (top), of which 5% or 50% are positive (blue). From the figure below, we can see that class imbalance causes ROC curves to be poor visualizations of classifier performance.

For instance, if only 5 out of 100 observations are positive, then we would expect the score of five positive rank top 5. If our classifier generates scores that rank these 5 cases as uniformly distributed in the top 15, the ROC graph will look good (a). However, if we had used a threshold such that the top 15 were predicted to be true, 10 of them would be FPs, which is not reflected in the ROC curve, means that the difference is more apparent in PR curve, which can be explained from the formulas.

So if our problem involves class imbalance and the positive class samples are very rare compared to the negative classes, precision recall curve would be a better choice.
